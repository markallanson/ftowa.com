If you think the car HAS done 60k then don't worry about replacing the cam belt yet, its not relaly due till 100K and i doubt you will be able to get the dealer to replace it before you buy.

Problems are generally minimal, support here in Perth is pretty strong now for any type of work that needs to be done on the FTO.

Make sure that the read suspension isn't making knocking sounds (droplinks). if it is, get them replaced before you buy.. check for panel damage, its rare to find one that hasn't been panel damaged (pull back the boot carpet near the corners at the back and see if you can see any spot weld type marks.

Take a manual GPX for a drive as well, and compare against the tiptronic box.

The manual is quicker, and speaking personally (mine's a manual) I'm happier with the simpler gearbox. If it goes wrong, it won't be as pricey to sort out. Of course, you could always get a warranty that might save your wallet.

Also, older tiptronics are 4-speed, I think - newer ones (1997 on?) are 5-speed. There is the odd 1997+ car around...

Look for accident damage front and rear... Paint overspray on the black rubber, the spoiler fixing screws and other places paint shouldn't be. Most FTOs I've seen (including mine) were bought in Japan as damaged vehicles and then fixed up... some better than others. Paint quality is the main deal, unless it's a really ham-fisted job.

What else? Hmm. Worn shocks... Bounce the front and rear, and ensure it doesn't keep bouncing.

Ask if the idle speed controller (stepper motor) has been replaced. The old models had dodgy aluminium housings that acted as heatsinks - fried the coil, and the car wouldn't idle at even rpm. If it's a '94, it's probably already died and been fixed with a decent replacement (bakelite housing).

Make sure there's a plastic pack in the boot with the jack stuff and a funny hexagonal extender thingy - this is a MIVEC spark plug remover, as they are recessed too deep for a standard socket set to reach. Not essential, but a pain if you don't have one and you want to do work yourself.

As Mark said, listen for funny knocks front and rear - things like droplinks, bushes can be worn. Not a big deal, but at least you'll know that it needs doing.

Check the aircon to make sure it blows cold.

Central locking can be a bit dodgy. The passenger side should lock/unlock in unison with the drivers side. If it doesn't, it's broke.

Listen to the engine once it's warmed up. If it's extremely 'tappetty', then for a GPX the valve clearances will need manual adjustment. For a GR, they are hydraulic lifters. There are 24 of them, so it's a fair job to sort out. I think the GPX recommendation is every 40,000km for this job.

And if it's straight from a dealer, the tyres will very likely be horrible, cheap, hard, crap rubber (Champiros or other dross). As it's getting towards winter, budget for some decent boots. Toyo Proxes T1-S, Pirelli P7000, etc. etc. etc. Those dodgy tyres will have you sliding badly in the wet. Scared the pants off me back when I got mine!

Phew!

Don't for a minute get scared off by the above list... This was everything I could think of. In 3 years of owning my '95 GPX, I've not had more than a $50 claim on my full dealer warranty. It's been a fantastically reliable car.

But a '94 model is now 9 years old, so just be aware of that.

Good luck!

- Rich
