To be honest, I'm not sure why you're looking to do this work... the FTO brakes are basically EVO I/II/III and do a cracking job.

The only things I could see being necessary are:

(1) Ability of the whole brake assembly to handle higher temps

or

(2) Larger disc area so they have greater ability to cool in moving air on track, ie. attempt to get around hitting (1) above.


For (1), you're talking about having a brake assembly where things like piston dust seals, fluid, etc. are able to see higher peak temperatures without going crispy/boily.

For (2), if you've experienced such a situation in an FTO (and want to not go there again), you might want to start by doing free/cheap stuff like cutting out the inadequate airflow vent slats in the front plastic wheel wells, fitting some simple 90-degree air duct tubing pointing at the calipers, and removing the dust guard metal. That last one involves a bit of hub disassembly, but it would certainly help.


So you'd have to determine when/where exactly you expect brake temperature problems to arise, what you can cheaply do to minimise it, whether you can simply change your approach towards brake temp management (ie. don't get your brakes glowing and then stop dead for a chat with someone, hehe!).


To give a real world example...

I had a good 15-minute non-stop thrash up and down the DTEC tarmac area, learning left-foot-braking, driving 9/10 last year. Big fun! I had stock GPX brakes, no duct holes cut out of the guards at that time and Mintex M1155 pads fitted. Not once did I get any brake fade at all - due to the pad material. Certainly no fluid boiling.

But what I *was* doing was getting the general front caliper assembly hotter than it was designed for, so while I had no brake fade or boiled fluid, I was cooking the piston dust seals.

I also made a critical mistake, in only performing a couple of cooldown laps when I should have taken it up and down the nearby road to really let the whole system get less hot. So when I stopped, that heat just soaked in, made crispy critters of my piston rubber dust seals, slightly boiled my (standard) fluid and made the remaining pad material rubbish bin material.

So looking at (1) above, I would need brakes as tough as nails to withstand parking up so soon after that kind of driving. Very expensive! The cheap option is to do some appropriate cool-down laps.

Looking at (2), larger discs would have transferred more heat to the airstream. But at that time, I hadn't looked into getting an effective airstream past the front wheels. Now I have, I know three things that can be done to get more air. If those don't do the trick, THEN the next option would be bigger rotors.


Note that we haven't touched on actual braking ability at all...! Because in my book the FTO brakes - with good maintenance, rotors and pads - are well balanced, have good pedal feel (dunno about ABS versions though) and are always capable of stopping the car as hard as your tyre rubber will allow.

I'm getting R-spec sticky tyres fitted for MC Motorsport training days soon, and I'm not expecting any brake limitations to be reached - I'm sure I'll still be able to touch the limits of traction (ie. lockup minus 1% ) time after time out there. But I'll post any interesting findings once I've given it a go!


Finally, be aware that changing calipers can sometimes introduce front/rear bias issues that need correcting. So investigate how the FTO brake bias valve stuff works, and if anything can be tweaked. The factory got it pretty right, I reckon, although dry/rain, good/bad tyres, etc. changes the likelihood of locking fronts or rears under threshold braking - for road cars, you're always looking at a "best average setup" really.


Hope this massive brain dump isn't (a) too inaccurate, (b) too stupid or (c) too boring to read!! Of course, feel free to rip strips off anything obviously BS. Physics is physics - if I don't understand the way something works, then let me know. That way, I'll know for next time... hehe.

- Rich

> However, after a short span of 8mths, they warped!

Track driving? ROAD driving?

After belting around and around DTEC tarmac area (short track, negligible straights), it makes you wonder just what people are doing to heat up brakes so hot on road driving that they transfer pad material unevenly!

I've had slotted DBA rotors (vented front) on mine since late 2001. Mucho motorkhana fun. The only time I've needed to skim them was after that time I mentioned, when I foolishly parked up while everything was still hot. Since then (October 2002) I've done a spirited MC Motorsport practice session at least once a month, smelt hot pads on numerous occasions, but always made sure I did the anal retentive cool-down parade to normalise temps if things were especially toasty. My rotors are now a bit blue, have a few stress cracks (2 years old now, and used HARD ), but no juddering brake horrors whatsoever.

To put things into perspective though, I don't do any full-course lap track days. A 48-65 second MC lap isn't going to get anything particularly hot. My road driving isn't going to melt pads or boil fluid - I don't live in the Darling Ranges or tow a double-wide in the FTO, hehe.

...and the only time I've ever smelt Mintex pads on the road has been when I was in the final stages of bedding them in. Zero-to-100-to-20-to-100-to-20-to-100...

- Rich

ps. I've actually moved from Mintex M1155 pad material to M1144 this year. This is now a LESS track-oriented compound... the idea being that a little feedback from the braking system saying "getting real hot now" in the form of initial fade is a GOOD thing, hehe. The M1155 stuff just kept on braking hard and sharp - perfect pedal feel, corner after corner - even though I was building up heat beyond sane levels. I must be getting old to think that less is more, hey!!!
